Granville Destresser Garden


Sensorial healing - Granville Park

This site is in granville park, near burrard and 16th. This area is just north of shaughnessy, and is surrounded by apartments and multi-family dwellings. On visits to the site we noticed alot of traffic noises. We could also smell the fuel and gas from the traffic. We noticed soft textures from the moss and interesting textures from the rock wall. The site also has long linear features which entices you to keep walking.

The purpose of this garden design is to create a healing garden. We specifically chose to heal stress because stress has been shown to been increasing for the past few years (pre-covid) and now stress is exacerbated with the pandemic and other factors such as family, relationships, finance, and work. Our design toolbox for healing stress will be built upon strategies from Attention Restoration Theory and Stress Recovery Theory. Our tools for design will be to subdivide space which creates engagement, to change perspective which creates fascination, and to create pleasant distractions by using sensorial plantings and changing topography.

We sketched some drafts which focused on dividing space with varying plantings on topographic changes. We investigated long and winding paths to keep users engaged with the site. The gardenâ€™s paths and planting grounds will rise and fall so the attraction is always changing. We took the idea of subdividing space and created a labyrinth. The difference between a maze and a labyrinth is that a maze has wrong paths and getting lost is guaranteed. A labyrinth is not meant to be a puzzle, but a form of physical meditation, as there is a continuous path from entrance to exit. In our final design, the labyrinth is divided in 3 sections with longitudinal paths that slope towards the east.
